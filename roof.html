Roof gardens

Roof gardens have existed since ancient times. Beyond just being aesthetically pleasing, roof gardens have practical benefits, too. Some environmental benefits of roof gardens include the following:

  • Thermal insulation (warms in winter and cools in summer)
  • Noise insulation
  • Wildlife habitats
  • Rainwater detention
  • Reduced air pollution (plants help trap particulates and gaseous emissions)

The following are some of the important criteria to consider before designing a roof garden:

  1. Roof characteristics/structure (it’s easier to design and construct a building with a roof garden in mind than to add a roof garden to an existing building, although that can also be done in many cases)
  2. Cost (will the benefits and overall savings outweigh the initial cost?)
  3. Climate (temperature, overall sun and shade exposure, wind, and so on)
  4. Design (how simple or elaborate will it be?)
  5. Purpose (is it just for residents to enjoy, or will guest tours be offered?)
  6. Maintenance (what is involved, what is the cost, who will take care of it?)

Roof gardens are becoming more popular as urban development depletes the few remaining green areas of many cities. Countless cities throughout the U.S. and around the world, in a variety of climates, are exploring the benefits of rooftop gardens. In both office and residential buildings, prospective tenants seem to consider roof gardens a worthwhile feature. One of the most elaborate examples of a roof garden is The Kensington Roof Gardens in London, England. It actually consists of three gardens: the Spanish Garden, the Woodland Garden, and the Tudor Garden. The gardens take up over one and a half acres and have a view of London’s skyline. Events, such as weddings, are held at the gardens frequently. A restaurant is also on the roof, plus a members’ club that is open on Friday and Saturday nights.
